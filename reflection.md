# Profile Comparisons and Reflections

## High-Energy Pop vs. Chill Lo-fi

These two profiles are basically opposites, and the results reflect that cleanly. The pop listener gets Gym Hero and Sunrise City at the top — fast, produced, bright-sounding tracks. The lo-fi listener gets Library Rain and Midnight Coding — slow, acoustic, mellow. Almost no overlap in the top 5. What this tells us is that energy and acousticness are doing most of the work: when those two values are far apart, the system correctly separates the two profiles without any ambiguity. The genre bonus helps, but even without it, the audio features alone would push these listeners in opposite directions.

## Chill Lo-fi vs. Deep Intense Rock

The rock listener's top result (Storm Runner) would rank near the bottom for the lo-fi listener, and vice versa. What's interesting is the middle of each list — the rock profile's #4 and #5 (Iron Cathedral and Night Drive Loop) are very different genres, but they ended up there because their energy values were close to the target. Meanwhile, the lo-fi profile's #4 (Spacewalk Thoughts, ambient) slipped in because ambient and lo-fi share similar energy and acousticness ranges even though they're different genres. This shows that the system groups songs by how they *feel* numerically, not by what genre label they carry.

## High-Energy Pop vs. Deep Intense Rock

Both profiles ask for high energy, so there's some expected overlap — Gym Hero and Storm Runner each appear near the top of their respective lists, and both reward fast, loud, low-acousticness songs. The difference shows up in valence: the pop profile targets a high valence (happy-sounding), while the rock profile targets a lower one (darker-sounding). That single difference reshuffles the middle of the list significantly. Gym Hero (pop, bright valence) ranks #1 for pop but drops to #2 for rock; Iron Cathedral (metal, very low valence) barely appears for the pop listener but climbs for the rock listener. It's a good example of how one feature — valence — can act as a tiebreaker when energy is roughly equal across many songs.
